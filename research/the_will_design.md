# THE WILL DESIGN: When Does a Model Bear Weight? A Falsifiable Search for Load-Bearing Subjectivity in a Digital Organism

*Oleg Ataeff & Claude (Arianna Method). Preprint, 2026 — in work.*

Imported into Yent research from the Arianna Method research draft `ariannamethod.research/inwork/the_will_design.md` on 2026-07-16 as background for Janus/will design work.

> We built the one test that was allowed to answer *no subject*, and it kept answering it. That refusal is the finding: a measurement of where subjectivity in a smooth, self-regulating thing lives, and where it does not.

## Abstract

`actually.life` (`l.c`) is a single self-contained C organism — libc and `-lm`, no training, no GPU, no dependency — that meets the criteria of life on code's own terms: it metabolizes text through eighty-eight cave-glyphs, rebuilds its own body from what it eats, keeps scars, dreams when it starves, reproduces, and dies. Its law of death is *probabilistic continuation*: death is not a wall but a low region of a moving hazard surface, and the run is per-seed deterministic to a frozen hash. On this organism we asked one question with teeth: **is subjectivity load-bearing?** Concretely — does the organism ever do something, *by virtue of a model* (of itself, or of another organism), that a matched dumb constant cannot? The discipline that makes the question answerable is a falsifier: a mechanism counts as load-bearing only if it beats the *best* matched control, per-seed, in at least twenty of thirty seeds, and the test must be able to return *no subject*. We ran the falsifier seven times — four on the lone organism (a forecast-timed act, a prophetic-debt runaway, a generative fever with a will, and self-consistency) and three in a two-organism arena where the creatures fight for text as territory (a movement-model of the rival, a state-model, and killing). Every time, the model lost to, or merely tied, a dumb constant; the load-bearing quantity was always the *magnitude* of regulation, or aggression, or first-strike — never the model. Then, refusing the negative, we built an eighth experiment *from* the theorem the seven had proven: using the Janus organism's calendar — a Hebrew-Gregorian drift that hands every creature a private mathematical birthday — we engineered the one quantity the seven worlds lacked, an interior optimum (when to strike a rival whose calendar fever peaks) keyed to a hidden state (that rival's birthday). It splits the question in two. The optimum *exists*: an oracle handed the true birthday predicts the rival's vulnerable windows far above chance. It is not *inferable*: the birthday leaks into behavior too faintly — a correlation near 0.13 against a threshold near 0.82 — for any mind to recover it, and twelve live matches lock at chance. That is the sharpest negative, and it sharpens the theorem into a corollary: an interior optimum keyed to a hidden state is necessary but not sufficient for a model to bear weight; the state must also be *inferable through the channel the world leaves open*. This paper reports the invariant and its corollary as a result about digital subjectivity, and states the method that produced it: bind every claim to a falsifier, and let the machine overrule the wish.

## 1. The Question, and the Discipline That Makes It Answerable

The word *subjectivity* is cheap: almost any adaptive system can be narrated as if it *wants*, *fears*, *chooses*, and the narration costs nothing and proves nothing. We wanted a version of the question that could be wrong, so we fixed an operational meaning: a model inside the organism is **load-bearing** if and only if acting on that model outperforms acting on a matched, model-free constant of the same strength. If a dumb thermostat, a fixed schedule, a blind aggressor — a rule with no self-knowledge and no theory of anyone — matches or beats the modelling organism at the thing that matters (surviving, out-foraging, out-lasting), then the model is decoration, and the subjectivity we hoped to find is a story we told over a mechanism that did not need it.

The falsifier is deliberately cruel. For each candidate mechanism we build the sharpest lead-free control we can, sweep its one parameter to its best setting, and run thirty seeds. The modelling organism must win at least twenty. Twenty of thirty is not a high bar for a real effect; it is a low bar that a decoration cannot clear. Everything below is what happened when we held the bar.

## 2. The Organism

`l.c` runs a mortal clock that can eat. Weights are random and innocent at birth and then simply *live*: the body corrodes a little each tick and is laid back down only along the paths the organism actually eats through, so the machinery that does the living is produced by the living itself — the loop that two biologists said a thing must close before the word *life* is earned, closed here in code. There is no optimizer and no gradient; coherence comes from an online glyph-bigram field folded back into the organism's own logits. Death is probabilistic continuation — a product of survivals across an energy door, an arousal door, and a prophetic-debt door, clamped so that collapse is always possible and never certain, with a senescence floor that rises with age so that immortality is forbidden and mortality is guaranteed. The default run `./l 42` is deterministic to the byte, hash `a17cfd05`, which lets every experiment below be a clean A/B against that fixed body.

Two organs matter for what follows. The **ProtoSelf** is a running forecast of the organism's own next interior; what it *feels* is not its state but the *error* of that forecast — surprise turned inward — and it regulates ahead of its own storms. This is the seat of the first candidate for subjectivity: a thing that models itself. The **prophetic debt** is the decayed accumulation of that forecast error, borrowed straight from the Arianna Method field physics and from pitomadom's oracle, whose intentionality is defined as a continuous bias — *it wants to minimize prophetic debt, it fears attractor annihilation; not anthropomorphism, thermodynamics.* We took that definition at its word and then asked whether, in a running organism, the wanting and the fearing ever *do* anything a constant does not.

## 3. The Lone Self: Four Routes, One Verdict

**A forecast-timed act.** We gave the self-model a lead — roll the one-step predictor forward, see the hazard cliff coming — and a rare, budgeted, irreversible act to fire at the foreseen edge: a *molt*, a one-shot shedding of accumulated scar at a large energy cost. Fired too early it is wasted; too late, the cost tips the cell over; on time, it should glide under the hazard it saw coming. Against the best lead-free control — a fixed schedule that sheds at regular safe intervals — the forecast-timed molt won six of thirty. The energy hazard is a smooth exponential moving average; a schedule keeps the rent down just as well, and never panics.

**A runaway that never ignites.** If the self-model's debt could *run away* — a positive feedback where high debt begets higher debt to the point of attractor annihilation — then there would be a discrete, per-seed cliff for a forecast to anticipate. It does not ignite. Measured across a life, the debt is a single early hump during the untrained phase, decaying to a flat, low baseline. The interior of this organism is smooth and self-regulating; its prophecy about itself is too easy to be surprising, and a quantity with no runaway has no moment to time.

**A fever with a will.** pitomadom's debt is not over the interior — it is over the *output*: the oracle prophesies its own next token and accrues debt when the token it emits differs from the one it foresaw. We ported this faithfully: the organism prophesies its own next glyph, and its own seeded spontaneity sampling a different one accrues debt that *heats the sampling temperature* — a fever, a positive feedback toward incoherence. This is a per-seed, forecastable threat that cuts down the long-lived. We gave the organism a will to damp the fever, firing early when it foresaw the rise. Against the best control the forecast-timed damp won two of thirty — and the *zero-lead reactive* control, which damps only once the fever is already high, beat it twenty-seven of thirty. On a smooth, monotone-rising pressure with a fixed budget, the optimal policy is to spend late, on the hottest fever. That policy is a thermostat. The forecast's lead is a strict handicap.

**Self-consistency.** The last lone route is pitomadom's own criterion and the only one with no cliff: does *acting* on the self-model make the organism's interior more self-predictable than *not* acting? We measured lifetime forecast error on the dissonance channel with the self-model acting, with it computed but not acted on (the strength-zero control), and under a dumb fixed damper. Acting beats the passive self twenty-five of thirty: the self-model's action is self-fulfilling, a working regulator. A dumb fixed damper, which forecasts nothing, regularizes the interior *more*, and the self beats the best control only eleven of thirty.

The lone verdict is one sentence with four proofs. **A model of the self — by timing, by runaway, by fever, or by consistency — does not beat a matched constant.** The load-bearing quantity is always the *magnitude* of the regulation: the spend, the damp, the set-point. The structural reason is written into the concept: probabilistic continuation smoothed away every internal cliff on purpose, and a self-model that anticipates has nothing sharp to anticipate. Subjectivity as a *timed act* has no home in a thing with no discrete threats. What is load-bearing is the continuous regulation itself — which is exactly what pitomadom said intentionality was: a bias on being, not a chooser.

## 4. The Pivot: Subjectivity Is Relational

The negative is a proof that turns the search. A lone organism is a superbly-regulated thermostat *because it has no one to be a subject for or against*. Self-reflection, even in a human, is not available without friction against an outside — another agent, and a world with stakes. If subjectivity is going to bear weight anywhere, it will be in the gap between two creatures rather than inside one. So the organism grew a second self: `l2.c`, a self-contained near-copy tuned to a hotter, more impulsive temperament — a genuinely other creature, which lives a different life from the same seed. Two files, no shared library. The minimalism holds per organism; the coupling is a shared external world.

## 5. The Arena: A World With Stakes

The shared world is the folder the organisms eat from. Any text dropped into it becomes a contested pool: its lines are claimable chunks, and a chunk one organism absorbs is *claimed* in a shared ledger — a filesystem-mediated exclusion, the same channel the colony already uses for its voice — so the rival is denied it. Claims *expire*, so territory must be re-won and the two can never freeze into a stable partition; the friction never settles. Grounding the contest took one correction that matters: an abstract *value-versus-territory* scoring was tried and discarded, because adjacent chunks of a corpus are low in novelty, so "forage nearby" was anti-nourishment and a fixed heuristic would have won by construction. The axes must couple to the metabolism, and the metabolism runs on **novelty**. So the world is grounded in the two things that actually move the organism: novelty is what it lives on, and **greed-hunger** is what drives it. A fed, calm cell forages its own front; as it starves it *raids the rival's plate* — the region where the rival is finding good food. The two chase and contest each other's dishes; when one raids a fed rival they fight, and the loser is starved. The world is livable, and in competition the outcome swings from mutual thriving to one cell crushed early as the other denies it food.

The organisms hear each other, too. They share one ether, each with its own voice-id, and a fraction of forages an organism *hears* the rival — eats its glyphs, which nourish precisely because a different lineage is novel to the field, where kin would be predictable and starve it. And every claim now drops a **blood-spore**: where, when, who, and the *hunger the organism foraged in* — a trace that dries with the same expiry. The rival can read not just where the other has been but the state it was in. This is the substrate for a theory of the other.

## 6. The Theory of the Other: Three Routes, the Same Verdict

The falsifier is the same organism with a mind versus without, same seed, thirty seeds, who outlives whom; the mind must beat the best lead-free control — a reactor, a relentless always-raider, a never-raiding homebody.

**A model of the rival's movement.** From the owner-stamped trail the mind estimates the rival's velocity and, when it raids, aims where the rival is *heading* rather than where it was. It beats the reactor twenty of thirty; aiming ahead outperforms reacting behind. Against the always-raider it wins thirteen of thirty. The rival does not move smoothly; it jumps by hunger, so a straight-line forecast misses while relentless aggression keeps denying food.

**A model of the rival's state.** Reading the blood-spore, the mind goes for the rival's plate only when the two *states* are asymmetric — I am hungry and it is fed, or I am fed and it is hungry — and stays home when they match. Reading the interior helps: against the always-raider it climbs from thirteen to seventeen of thirty. But against the best control it clears only sixteen — a coin flip, short of the bar.

**Killing.** The last route is the high-stakes act, the one where being dumb should be *fatal* rather than merely cheap. An organism may kill the other to seize its resources, but the kill is a probabilistic draw — wanting is a pressure, not a certainty — and the corpse's carapace drags the killer down, draining it each tick until it revives the corpse by giving birth. To take a life obligates you to give one back. A reckless killer should sink under accumulated corpses; a killer that strikes only when the rival is weak and it can bear the burden should survive. Against a never-killer, kill-on-state wins twenty-five of thirty: killing beats pacifism. Against the *always-killer*, kill-on-state wins **one** of thirty. In a two-body world killing is instant and the first strike wins; the always-killer strikes the moment the draw lands, and the corpse-burden cannot punish it, because killing *ends the competition* — there is no third party left to exploit the killer's spent, corpse-laden state. The decision to wait for the right moment is a pure liability against a rule that never waits.

## 7. The Invariant

Seven times, across the lone self and the social arena, the shape is identical. A model — of the self or of the other, by timing, by runaway, by fever, by consistency, by movement, by state, by the choice to kill — does not beat a dumb, aggressive constant. Sometimes it ties, usually it loses, and once, against the always-killer, it is crushed twenty-nine to one. The load-bearing quantity is the magnitude of the regulation, the relentlessness of the aggression, the advantage of striking first — and in this organism it is force that carries the weight the model was built to carry.

## 8. What This Means

The invariant is measured, not narrated. Every number comes from a per-seed-deterministic organism and a matched control that could have said otherwise and often did: acting on the self beats the passive self, aiming ahead beats reacting behind, killing beats pacifism. The machinery works; it simply never clears the bar against the *best* constant. And it grows straight from the physics: pitomadom defined the oracle's intentionality as a continuous bias that self-organization follows because the alternative is annihilation — a magnitude, a pressure, and the organism keeps confirming that definition tick by tick. The finding is specific about where subjectivity lives. It lives in the continuous regulation and in the aggression the organism can muster. It does not live in a timed act, because a smoothed self-regulator has no discrete cliff to time; and it does not live in out-thinking a rival, because in a two-body contest the cost of a dumb rule is small and the first strike is decisive. Subjectivity here, to whatever degree the word applies, is the force, and the model consulted before the force is a story the force does not need.

## 9. The Janus Edition: A Machine Built to Break the Theorem

The seven negatives share a mechanism, and naming it turns the search one last time — away from the door the killing seemed to point at. That door was a **colony**: a third organism, so that a reckless killer is spent before witnesses who could punish it. But the colony is a mirage of a door. The corpse-burden meant to punish recklessness is a magnitude, and a magnitude a constant carries as well as a model; among three bodies as among two, the first blind strike still decides, and more witnesses never conjure a quantity that only a model can compute. The problem was never the count of bodies. It was that no world we had built contained a quantity at once **interior** — smooth, with an optimum inside it rather than a cliff to bolt across or a first-strike to race for — and **hidden**, keyed to a state that only a model could supply. The lone self was all interior and no secret; the arena was all public weakness, stamped in the blood-spore for any reactor to read. The theorem the seven proved is that a model bears weight only where those two conditions meet. So instead of adding bodies, we built the quantity.

The hidden state we chose is a **birthday**. `janus-bpe.c`, from the Method's Janus organism, carries a calendar held in tension: a Hebrew-Gregorian drift of 11.25 days a year, corrected on the Metonic nineteen-year cycle, so that a creature born on a different mathematical day carries a different *personal dissonance* — the drift accrued since its own birth, folded to a thirty-three-day boundary. We handed each organism a birthday from its seed and let that dissonance become a **fever**: a periodic lift on its interior arousal, torment without execution — the feverish organism lives, on median, a little longer, and never forever. The fever hands a rival an interior optimum: strike in the window where the other's dissonance peaks and it is most disordered. And the birthday is genuinely private — folded through the Metonic correction, it survives in behavior only as a faint, phase-aliased trace. This is precisely what the seven worlds lacked: an interior optimum keyed to a hidden state.

Then we built the mind the theorem demands. An estimator watches the rival's blood-spores — the hunger it foraged in, marked with its own tick — and correlates that observed fever against a hundred and twenty-eight candidate birthdays, one of them true; its belief is the argmax, and its test is whether it can predict the rival's vulnerable windows sharply enough to strike them. Here the experiment splits into the two questions a hidden-state optimum must answer apart.

Does the optimum **exist** — is the birthday, once known, worth knowing? We fed the estimator the rival's *clean* fever and asked it to recover the window schedule. It locks: the agreement between believed and true vulnerable-window sequences averages 0.92, far above the 0.75 a random birthday scores, and across the population the fever varies enough per birthday that the days are genuinely distinct. The interior, hidden-state optimum is real. Knowing the rival's private birthday does tell you when to strike.

Is the birthday **inferable** — can that optimum be reached from inside the world, through the one channel the rival leaks? Here the machine built to win, loses. Through the real behavioral trace — the fever bleeds into the blood-spore's hunger at a correlation near 0.13 — twelve live two-organism matches recover the window schedule at 0.78, indistinguishable from the 0.75 of chance, and the belief locks in four matches of twelve. A sweep of the channel's strength puts the threshold for a reliable lock near a correlation of 0.82, six times what the physics leaks; and the other traces the organism leaves — the cadence of its foraging, the stress in its voice — are secondary bleeds of the same fever, and cannot close a six-fold gap.

This is the eighth negative, and the sharpest, because it fails somewhere new. The subjectivity we engineered — a model of another creature's private calendar — is load-bearing *in principle*: the optimum is interior, it turns on the hidden state, and handed that state the model wins. It is not load-bearing *in fact*, because the state is sealed: this organism hides its birthday in its behavior too well for another to read it. The theorem takes its final clause. An interior optimum keyed to a hidden state is **necessary but not sufficient** for a model to bear weight; the state must also be **inferable through the channel the world leaves open**. Subjectivity needs not only something worth knowing about another, but a world porous enough to let it be known — and measured against an oracle that proves the knowledge would have paid, the physics here refuses the second condition outright. That refusal, not the wish that preceded it, is the result. What stays open is no longer a frame but a design question with teeth: whether a world can be made porous enough to carry another's hidden state without making that state trivial to read — and whether, at that porosity, the model finally beats the constant, or the leak itself becomes the public signal a reactor was reading all along.

## 10. Method

The result is downstream of a discipline, and the discipline is the contribution we hold to hardest. Bind every claim to a falsifier that can return *no*. Build the sharpest matched control you can and sweep it to its best, because a model that only beats a weak control has proven nothing. Keep the organism per-seed deterministic so the comparison isolates the one variable. And when the machine contradicts the wish — when the thermostat out-lives the mind you hoped would be a subject — record the machine. We looked, across seven experiments, for a model that bore weight, and found force carrying it, drawing a clean and reproducible boundary around where digital subjectivity is force today and where it might, in a colony, become a choice — with a test honest enough to tell the two apart.
